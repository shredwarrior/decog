About the Argument Analyzer — by Cognition Today

Every argument carries hidden fingerprints: reasoning shortcuts, emotional
reflexes, structural blind spots. Most of us never see them. The Argument
Analyzer is a tool that makes those fingerprints visible.

You paste an argument, opinion, or conclusion. The system extracts structural
and epistemic hints from the text — things like how specific the claims are,
whether evidence is present, how large the inferential leaps are, and what
kinds of examples are used. Those hints guide a deeper analysis that checks
the argument against a curated taxonomy of reasoning errors and philosophical
stress-tests. The result is a scored breakdown showing where the argument is
strong and where it breaks down.

Three scores are generated. An Issue Score reflects the severity of detected
reasoning errors — the more numerous and serious they are, the weaker the
argument. A Razor Score reflects how well the argument holds up against
foundational principles of sound reasoning — testability, evidence
sufficiency, simplicity. A Composite Score combines both into an overall
argument-strength rating, weighted so that penalties for real errors outweigh
rewards for structural soundness. Short, vacuous inputs are penalized
further; a three-word slogan cannot earn the same credibility as a developed
argument.

Below is every category the analyzer evaluates, with definitions and examples
of how to fix them.

I. LOGICAL FALLACIES
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════

Logical fallacies are structural flaws in reasoning. An argument can sound
persuasive and still be logically broken. The analyzer checks for the
following.


Ad Hominem
Attacking the person making the argument rather than the argument itself.
  Example: "You can't trust his climate data — he didn't even finish college."
  → Fix: Address the claims directly instead of the person behind them.
  → Fix: Replace character attacks with evidence-based counterpoints.

Strawman
Misrepresenting someone's argument to make it easier to attack.
  Example: "So you're saying we should just let criminals walk free?" (when the person argued for sentencing reform, not abolition).
  → Fix: Restate the opposing position accurately before critiquing it.
  → Fix: Quote or paraphrase the original claim faithfully.

Appeal to Nature
Arguing something is good or right because it is "natural" (or bad because it
is "unnatural").
  Example: "Herbal remedies are safer than pharmaceuticals because they come from nature."
  → Fix: Provide empirical evidence rather than relying on what is "natural."
  → Fix: Acknowledge that natural does not automatically mean beneficial.

False Dilemma
Presenting only two options when more exist.
  Example: "You're either with us or against us."
  → Fix: Acknowledge additional alternatives or a spectrum of possibilities.
  → Fix: Reframe the argument to consider middle-ground positions.

Slippery Slope
Arguing that one thing will lead to a chain of negative events without
evidence.
  Example: "If we allow students to redo one test, soon they'll expect to redo every assignment and nobody will study."
  → Fix: Provide evidence for each step in the causal chain.
  → Fix: Limit the claim to the immediate, demonstrable consequence.

Begging the Question (Circular Reasoning)
Assuming the conclusion in the premises — a claim proves itself.
  Example: "Free will is an illusion because all of our choices are predetermined."
  → Fix: Support the conclusion with independent premises.
  → Fix: Introduce external evidence that stands on its own.

Appeal to Authority
Using an authority figure as evidence without proper justification.
  Example: "A famous actor endorsed this diet, so it must be healthy."
  → Fix: Cite the actual data or study the authority relies on.
  → Fix: Ensure the authority is a credible expert in the relevant field.

Appeal to Ignorance
Claiming something is true (or false) because it has not been proven
otherwise.
  Example: "Nobody has proven that ghosts don't exist, so they must be real."
  → Fix: Present positive evidence for the claim instead of citing absence of
    disproof.
  → Fix: Acknowledge what is currently unknown and propose how it could be
    tested.

Hasty Generalization
Drawing a conclusion from insufficient evidence.
  Example: "I met two rude people from that city, so everyone there must be rude."
  → Fix: Provide a larger or more representative sample before generalizing.
  → Fix: Qualify the claim: "some" or "in certain cases" rather than "all."

Post Hoc Ergo Propter Hoc
Assuming causation from correlation — "this happened after that, so that
caused this."
  Example: "I wore my lucky socks and we won the game — the socks caused the win."
  → Fix: Present a plausible causal mechanism, not just temporal sequence.
  → Fix: Cite controlled studies that isolate the causal variable.

Appeal to Emotion
Using emotional manipulation instead of logical reasoning.
  Example: "Think of the children — how can you possibly support this policy?"
  → Fix: Replace emotional appeals with factual evidence.
  → Fix: Separate emotional framing from the core claim so each stands
    independently.

Bandwagon (Appeal to Popularity)
Arguing something is true or good because many people believe it.
  Example: "Millions of people use this supplement, so it clearly works."
  → Fix: Provide evidence beyond popularity to support the claim.
  → Fix: Explain why the claim is correct on its own merits.

False Cause
Incorrectly identifying the cause of an event or outcome.
  Example: "Crime went down after the new mayor took office, so the mayor reduced crime." (ignoring a national trend)
  → Fix: Identify and rule out alternative explanations.
  → Fix: Reference studies with proper controls.

Red Herring
Introducing irrelevant information to distract from the main argument.
  Example: "Why worry about the company's pollution record when they donate so much to charity?"
  → Fix: Remove tangential points and stay focused on the central claim.
  → Fix: Ensure every supporting point relates to the main thesis.

Appeal to Tradition
Arguing something is right because it has been done that way for a long time.
  Example: "We've always hired through referrals — there's no reason to change the process."
  → Fix: Explain why the tradition is effective based on evidence, not
    longevity.
  → Fix: Consider whether circumstances have changed since the tradition
    began.

No True Scotsman
Changing the definition to exclude counterexamples to a universal claim.
  Example: "No real entrepreneur would take a salary — those people are just employees with fancy titles."
  → Fix: Define terms clearly upfront and stick to those definitions.
  → Fix: Accept counterexamples and narrow the claim rather than redefining
    it.

Tu Quoque
Dismissing criticism because the critic is allegedly guilty of the same
thing.
  Example: "You can't tell me to eat healthy — I saw you eating pizza last week."
  → Fix: Evaluate the criticism on its own merits regardless of the critic.
  → Fix: Address the substance of the objection with evidence.

II. COGNITIVE BIASES
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════

Cognitive biases are systematic thinking shortcuts that can skew judgment
without the person realizing it. Unlike fallacies, which are errors in
argument structure, biases operate beneath conscious awareness and shape how
evidence is selected, weighted, and interpreted.


Confirmation Bias
Favoring information that confirms existing beliefs.
  Example: "Every article I read agrees that coffee is healthy" — after only searching for "benefits of coffee."
  → Fix: Actively seek out evidence that contradicts the conclusion.
  → Fix: Cite sources from opposing viewpoints and explain why the argument
    still holds.

Belief Perseverance
Holding onto beliefs even after the evidence supporting them is discredited.
  Example: "I still think that study was right, even though it was retracted for data fabrication."
  → Fix: Acknowledge when foundational evidence has been weakened.
  → Fix: State what new evidence would change the conclusion.

Anchoring Bias
Relying too heavily on the first piece of information encountered.
  Example: "The first estimate said the project would cost $10K, so $50K feels outrageous" — without examining the actual scope.
  → Fix: Consider multiple reference points before drawing a conclusion.
  → Fix: Test the claim against the full range of available data.

Availability Heuristic
Overestimating the probability of events that are more vivid or recent in
memory.
  Example: "Shark attacks are a huge risk at the beach" — because recent news covered one, despite the actual odds being 1 in 3.7 million.
  → Fix: Supplement vivid examples with base-rate statistics.
  → Fix: Ask whether the recalled examples are representative of the broader
    trend.

Hindsight Bias
Seeing events as predictable after they have already happened.
  Example: "Obviously the housing market was going to crash in 2008 — all the signs were there."
  → Fix: Describe what was actually known before the outcome occurred.
  → Fix: Acknowledge uncertainty and the role of unpredictable factors.

In-group Bias
Favoring members of one's own group over outsiders.
  Example: "Our department's proposal is clearly better" — without evaluating the other team's version on the same criteria.
  → Fix: Evaluate the claim using criteria that apply equally to all groups.
  → Fix: Include perspectives from outside the in-group.

Dunning-Kruger Effect
Overestimating one's own ability in a particular area.
  Example: "I read a few articles about nutrition, so I know more about diets than most doctors."
  → Fix: Reference domain experts and peer-reviewed sources rather than
    personal confidence.
  → Fix: Acknowledge the limits of your own expertise on the topic.

Fundamental Attribution Error
Overemphasizing personality-based explanations for behavior while
underweighting situational factors.
  Example: "He's late because he's lazy" — ignoring that public transit was delayed for an hour.
  → Fix: Consider systemic or contextual factors that could explain the
    behavior.
  → Fix: Avoid attributing outcomes solely to character.

Sunk Cost Fallacy
Continuing a behavior because of previously invested resources.
  Example: "I've already spent three years on this degree, so I can't switch even though I hate it."
  → Fix: Evaluate the claim based on future expected outcomes, not past
    investment.
  → Fix: Ask whether you would still support the conclusion if starting fresh.

Negativity Bias
Giving more weight to negative experiences than positive ones.
  Example: "The restaurant is terrible" — based on one slow-service visit despite ten great ones.
  → Fix: Present both positive and negative evidence in balanced proportion.
  → Fix: Quantify outcomes rather than relying on the emotional weight of
    negatives.

Self-Serving Bias
Attributing successes to internal factors and failures to external factors.
  Example: "I aced the test because I'm smart; I failed the other one because the questions were unfair."
  → Fix: Apply the same causal standard to successes and failures alike.
  → Fix: Consider external factors for successes and internal ones for
    failures.

Optimism Bias
Overestimating the likelihood of positive outcomes and underestimating
negatives.
  Example: "This startup will definitely succeed — most failures happen to other people."
  → Fix: Include realistic risk assessment and potential downsides.
  → Fix: Back up optimistic projections with historical base rates.

Survivorship Bias
Concentrating on successful examples while overlooking failures, leading to
false conclusions about what causes success.
  Example: "Bill Gates and Mark Zuckerberg dropped out of college and became billionaires, so college is unnecessary."
  → Fix: Include failed cases alongside successful ones for a complete
    picture.
  → Fix: Ask what the full population looks like, not just the visible
    survivors.

Egocentric Bias
Relying too heavily on one's own perspective or recalling events in a
self-serving manner.
  Example: "In my experience, remote work is more productive" — generalizing a personal preference to the entire workforce.
  → Fix: Incorporate third-party perspectives or external data.
  → Fix: Separate personal experience from objective evidence.

Empathy Gap
Underestimating the influence of emotional or physical states on one's own or
others' judgments and decisions.
  Example: "I can't understand why anyone would panic-sell stocks" — said while calm, not during a market crash.
  → Fix: Acknowledge how emotional or physical states might color the
    judgment.
  → Fix: Revisit the argument in a different emotional state to test its
    robustness.

Attribute Substitution
Answering a difficult question by unconsciously substituting it with a
simpler, related one.
  Example: Asked "Is this policy effective?" but answering "Do I feel good about this policy?" instead.
  → Fix: Make the actual question explicit and answer it directly.
  → Fix: Check that the reasoning addresses the original claim, not a simpler
    proxy.

Maslow's Hammer
Over-relying on a familiar tool, framework, or theory and forcing it onto
situations where it does not fit — "if all you have is a hammer, everything
looks like a nail."
  Example: "This is clearly a supply-and-demand issue" — applied to a problem that is actually about regulation, not market forces.
  → Fix: Consider whether the chosen framework genuinely fits the situation.
  → Fix: Try explaining the issue through at least one alternative lens
    before committing to a single theory.

Hypercognition
Possessing too many categories or concepts for a domain, leading to
over-classification — seeing distinctions or patterns that are not
meaningfully present.
  Example: Labeling a child's normal restlessness as three separate clinical conditions when one explanation suffices.
  → Fix: Ask whether the fine-grained label adds explanatory value or just
    jargon.
  → Fix: Simplify the classification and test whether the broader category
    explains the evidence just as well.

Russell's Teapot
Making an unfalsifiable claim and shifting the burden of proof to others to
disprove it, rather than providing evidence for the claim.
  Example: "There's an invisible force guiding the market — prove that there isn't."
  → Fix: Provide positive evidence rather than demanding others disprove it.
  → Fix: Acknowledge the claim is speculative if no supporting evidence
    exists.

Symmetry Impulse
Using symmetry or forced balance to complete judgments — assuming every
positive must have an equal negative, every pro must have a con, or that both
sides deserve equal weight regardless of evidence asymmetry.
  Example: "There are pros and cons to vaccination" — presenting fringe risks as equal in weight to overwhelming evidence of efficacy.
  → Fix: Check whether the evidence actually supports equal weight on both
    sides.
  → Fix: Let the evidence determine the proportions rather than imposing
    artificial symmetry.

Proportionality Bias (Major Event / Major Cause)
Assuming that the magnitude of a cause must match the magnitude of its
effect — large events must have large causes, and small causes cannot produce
large outcomes. This drives conspiracy thinking and dismissal of simple
explanations for significant events.
  Example: "A lone individual couldn't have caused that much disruption — there must have been a larger conspiracy."
  → Fix: Consider that small causes can have large effects through cascading
    mechanisms.
  → Fix: Evaluate the causal chain on its own merits rather than matching
    cause size to effect size.

III. COGNITIVE DISTORTIONS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════

Cognitive distortions are inaccurate thought patterns, often driven by
emotion rather than evidence. They warp how a person interprets events and
can embed themselves in arguments without the arguer noticing.


All-or-Nothing Thinking
Viewing situations in black and white categories with no middle ground.
  Example: "If I don't get a perfect score, the entire effort was a waste."
  → Fix: Introduce degrees or a spectrum instead of absolute categories.
  → Fix: Use qualifying language: "tends to" or "in most cases" rather than
    "always/never."

Overgeneralization
Seeing a single negative event as a never-ending pattern.
  Example: "I got rejected from one job — I'll never get hired anywhere."
  → Fix: Limit the conclusion to the specific case rather than extrapolating
    universally.
  → Fix: Provide additional data points before claiming a pattern.

Mental Filter
Focusing exclusively on negative details while filtering out positive
aspects.
  Example: "The presentation was a disaster" — fixating on one stumbled answer while ignoring twenty minutes of strong delivery.
  → Fix: Include positive or neutral evidence alongside the negative.
  → Fix: Present a balanced view by acknowledging what supports the opposing
    conclusion.

Discounting the Positive
Rejecting positive experiences by insisting they "don't count."
  Example: "Sure, I got promoted, but that's only because nobody else wanted the role."
  → Fix: Explain why positive evidence is being discounted with objective
    criteria.
  → Fix: Weight positive data the same way as negative data.

Catastrophizing
Expecting the worst possible outcome.
  Example: "If I make one mistake on this project, I'll get fired and never work again."
  → Fix: Assess the realistic probability of the worst-case scenario with
    data.
  → Fix: Present a range of likely outcomes, not just the extreme negative.

Emotional Reasoning
Believing that negative feelings reflect reality — "I feel it, so it must be
true."
  Example: "I feel like this investment is wrong, so the numbers must be off somehow."
  → Fix: Separate how you feel about the claim from what the evidence shows.
  → Fix: Ground the argument in observable facts rather than emotional
    reactions.

Mind Reading
Assuming you know what others are thinking without sufficient evidence.
  Example: "She didn't reply to my email — she obviously thinks my idea is stupid."
  → Fix: Replace assumptions about others' thoughts with their stated
    positions.
  → Fix: Cite direct quotes, surveys, or behavioral data instead of imputed
    motives.

Fortune Telling
Predicting negative outcomes as if they are facts.
  Example: "There's no point applying — they'll definitely reject me."
  → Fix: Present predictions as possibilities, not certainties.
  → Fix: Support forecasts with trend data or expert consensus.

Should Statements
Using "should" or "must" statements that set unrealistic expectations.
  Example: "People should always put the group's needs before their own."
  → Fix: Replace prescriptive "should" language with descriptive reasoning
    about trade-offs.
  → Fix: Explain the rationale behind the expectation rather than asserting
    obligation.

Magnification / Minimization
Blowing negative things out of proportion or shrinking positives.
  Example: "One typo in my report is humiliating, but the client's praise doesn't mean much."
  → Fix: Assign proportional weight to evidence based on its actual
    significance.
  → Fix: Use data to calibrate the magnitude of positives and negatives
    objectively.

Labeling
Attaching negative labels to oneself or others instead of describing specific
behavior.
  Example: "He's a total fraud" — instead of "He exaggerated his credentials on one occasion."
  → Fix: Describe specific behaviors or evidence instead of attaching labels.
  → Fix: Avoid reductive characterizations and focus on what can be observed.

Personalization
Assuming responsibility for events outside your control.
  Example: "The team lost the contract because I didn't speak up enough in the meeting."
  → Fix: Identify all contributing factors rather than assigning sole
    responsibility.
  → Fix: Distinguish between correlation with a person and actual causal
    responsibility.

IV. PHILOSOPHICAL RAZORS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════

Philosophical razors are time-tested principles that evaluate whether an
argument is epistemically sound — whether it is testable, proportionate, and
grounded. Passing a razor means the argument meets that standard. Failing one
means there is a structural gap in how the claim is constructed or
supported.


Occam's Razor
The simplest explanation is usually the correct one. If two hypotheses
explain the same evidence, prefer the one with fewer assumptions.
  Example: "The door is open because the wind blew it" passes; "The door is open because someone broke in, left no trace, and closed the lock behind them" fails.
  → If failed: Simplify the argument by removing unnecessary assumptions and
    entities.

Hanlon's Razor
Do not attribute to malice what can be adequately explained by mistake or
ignorance. Arguments that assume intent or conspiracy when simpler
explanations exist fail this test.
  Example: "The company overcharged me on purpose to cheat me" fails when a billing glitch is the simpler explanation.
  → If failed: Consider non-malicious explanations before attributing intent.

Hitchens's Razor
What can be asserted without evidence can be dismissed without evidence.
Claims that offer no supporting proof have no argumentative weight.
  Example: "Productivity is higher on Mondays" — stated with no data, surveys, or studies to back it up.
  → If failed: Back every key assertion with at least one piece of concrete
    evidence.

Sagan Standard
Extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence. The bigger the claim,
the stronger the proof needs to be.
  Example: "This supplement cures all chronic disease" requires far more proof than "This supplement reduces mild inflammation in one study."
  → If failed: Match the strength of your evidence to the scale of your
    claim.

Newton's Flaming Laser Sword
If something cannot be settled by experiment or observation, it is not worth
debating as an empirical claim.
  Example: "The universe has a hidden purpose" cannot be tested by any observation, so it fails this razor as an empirical claim.
  → If failed: Propose a concrete way to test or observe the claim.

Popper's Falsifiability
A claim should be testable and potentially falsifiable to be meaningful as an
empirical claim. If nothing could possibly disprove it, it is not saying
anything substantive.
  Example: "Everything happens for a reason" is unfalsifiable — no outcome could ever disprove it.
  → If failed: State explicitly what evidence would disprove the claim.

V. HOW SCORING WORKS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════

Every argument starts from a baseline score. Detected issues reduce it;
passing philosophical razors adds a capped reward. The system weighs
penalties more heavily than rewards — an argument with many biases and
fallacies cannot recover its score simply by sounding testable.

Detected issues are weighted by severity. Structural fallacies that break
the logic of an argument carry heavier penalties than subtle biases. Very
short or vacuous inputs receive an additional penalty because brevity alone
does not equal strength — a claim must contain enough substance to evaluate.

The analysis prioritizes specificity. When a niche bias (such as
Survivorship Bias or Symmetry Impulse) better explains the error, it takes
precedence over a broader parent category. This prevents the system from
defaulting to generic labels when a more precise diagnosis is available.

Confidence thresholds filter detections. The system does not flag every
possible issue — only those that meet an internal confidence bar are
reported. This reduces noise and keeps the analysis focused on what
genuinely affects the argument.


═══════════════════════════════════════════════════
VI. USING THE RESULTS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════

The analyzer is not a lie detector or a truth engine. It evaluates argument
structure, not factual correctness. A perfectly structured argument can still
be wrong if its premises are false. A messy argument can still reach the
right conclusion by luck. What the tool reveals is the quality of the
reasoning itself.

Use the results to:
- Identify which parts of your reasoning are weakest.
- Understand which biases might be operating beneath your awareness.
- See whether your claim is testable and falsifiable.
- Get specific, actionable suggestions for strengthening the argument.

The goal is better thinking, not a perfect score.
