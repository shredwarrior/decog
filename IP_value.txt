1. ARGUMENT PERSONA SIGNATURES
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Concept:
  Arguments carry structural signatures that characterize the author type.
  These signatures emerge from the per-claim metadata profile — not from
  WHAT is argued, but from HOW reasoning is structured, where evidence
  appears, where it's absent, and how quality varies across claims.

Proposed persona categories:
  - AI-generated
  - Novice / untrained
  - Domain expert
  - Intelligent non-expert (cross-domain reasoner)
  - Rhetorically skilled (persuasive but potentially manipulative)

Key signature dimensions (derived from existing 23-field metadata):

  DIMENSION                         AI      NOVICE   EXPERT   INT. NON-EXPERT
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Avg causal chain length           High    Low      High     Moderate
  Variance in causal chain          Low     Low      High     Moderate
  Emotional tone distribution       Flat    Skewed   Neutral  Mixed
  Absolute language %               Low     High     Low      Low-moderate
  Evidence type mix                 Uniform None/Anec Scientific Analogical
  Exemplar type                     Generic Anecdote Pop. data Cross-domain
  Avg inferential gap               Small   Large    Small    Medium
  Variance in inferential gap       Low     Low      High     Moderate
  Avg speculation level             Low     High     Low      Moderate
  Assumes intent %                  Low     High     Low      Moderate
  Generalizes %                     Low     High     Low      Moderate
  Face validity consistency         Uniform Uniform  Bimodal  Gradient
  Specificity                       High    Low      High     Medium-high
  Verifiability                     High    Low      High     Mixed

  KEY INSIGHT: VARIANCE IS THE DISCRIMINATOR.
  - AI arguments are too uniform — every claim is roughly the same quality.
    Real humans have peaks and valleys.
  - Novice arguments are also uniform — but uniformly weak.
  - Expert arguments are bimodal — claims in their domain are exceptional,
    claims outside it may be casual assertions.
  - Intelligent non-experts show a gradient — quality degrades as they move
    further from their core knowledge.

  This variance pattern is the strongest differentiator between AI and
  human-authored arguments. No existing AI detection tool uses this
  approach.

Implementation path:
  Phase 1 (now):      Persist per-claim metadata in database. Feature vector
                      is computed at analysis time. No profiling yet — just
                      accumulate data.
  Phase 2 (month 2):  Unsupervised clustering (k-means / DBSCAN) on feature
                      vectors to discover natural argument groupings. See
                      what emerges WITHOUT predefining categories.
  Phase 3 (month 3+): Collect optional labels from users ("I wrote this" /
                      "AI wrote this" / "I'm a student" / "I'm a professional").
                      Map labels onto discovered clusters. Some clusters may
                      align with expected categories; others may reveal
                      groupings we didn't anticipate.
  Phase 4:            Surface persona label on the analysis report, derived
                      from the cluster the argument falls into. The label
                      describes the cluster's characteristics, not a
                      predetermined archetype.

  CRITICAL: Personas are derived from data, not hard-coded. The table of
  expected dimensions above (AI=uniform, expert=bimodal, etc.) is a
  hypothesis to be tested against the clusters, not a template to match.

Defensibility:
  - The persona concept + variance-based profiling is novel and publishable.
  - The discovered clusters are empirical findings, not assumptions —
    harder to replicate than copying a predefined taxonomy.
  - The labeled dataset grows with every user, compounding the moat.
  - No competitor has the metadata pipeline to compute these features.

2a. SYMMETRY IMPULSE (published by Aditya)
    Status: IMPLEMENTED. Published on Cognition Today:
      https://cognitiontoday.com/symmetry-impulse-why-how-we-seek-symmetry/
    Added to definitions.json as "symmetry_impulse" (penalty -8).

    Concept: People use symmetry to complete judgments — assuming every
    positive must have an equal negative, every pro must have a con, or
    both sides of an issue deserve equal weight regardless of evidence
    asymmetry. Driven by perceptual fluency and compression: symmetry is
    the brain's fastest way to impose structure on uncertainty.

    Types include valence symmetry (pro-con balancing), cause-effect
    symmetry (proportional causes), extrapolation symmetry, directional
    symmetry, and social symmetry.

    Detection signal (metadata):
    - forces_balance = true (new Call 1 tag): claim imposes artificial
      symmetry despite evidence asymmetry.
    - Metadata flag fires when any claim has forces_balance = true,
      nudging Call 2 toward Symmetry Impulse detection.

    Persona relevance:
    - AI-generated text is heavily trained toward symmetry ("there are
      arguments on both sides..."). Detecting forced symmetry is a strong
      AI signature signal.
    - Experts rarely use false balance — they state what the evidence
      shows and don't artificially balance it.
    - Rhetorically skilled arguers may use false balance strategically.

    IP value: HIGH. This is a named heuristic with published priority.
    This tool is the ONLY system that can flag this pattern.

2a-ii. PROPORTIONALITY BIAS / MEMC (published by Aditya)
    Status: IMPLEMENTED. Published on Cognition Today:
      https://cognitiontoday.com/why-we-justify-big-events-with-big-causes-proportionality-bias/
    Added to definitions.json as "proportionality_bias" (penalty -8).

    Concept: People assume the magnitude of a cause must match the
    magnitude of its effect (Major Event / Major Cause heuristic). Large
    events must have large causes; small causes cannot produce large
    outcomes. Drives conspiracy thinking and dismissal of simple
    explanations for significant events.

    Detection signal (metadata):
    - proportional_causation = true (new Call 1 tag): claim assumes
      cause-effect proportionality without evidence.
    - Metadata flag fires when any claim has this tag, nudging Call 2
      toward Proportionality Bias detection.

    Persona relevance:
    - Common in conspiracy-minded arguments and emotionally charged
      reasoning about large-scale events.
    - Experts and scientists are trained to evaluate mechanisms rather
      than match magnitudes (the butterfly effect principle).

    IP value: HIGH. Published priority + novel detection mechanism.

2b. EXTENSIBLE TAXONOMY: FROM ERROR DETECTION TO THINKING PATTERN PROFILING

    Core insight: the metadata extraction layer (Call 1) is content-agnostic.
    It tags structural features of claims. It doesn't care whether those
    features are used to detect errors or to profile thinking strategies.

    The system can be extended from "find what's wrong" to "characterize
    how this person thinks" by adding new metadata tags to Call 1:

    Proposed thinking-pattern tags (beyond current error-focused tags):
      - uses_analogy            : bool (reasons by analogy?)
      - analogical_domain       : string (source domain of the analogy)
      - hedges_appropriately    : bool (qualifies certainty?)
      - acknowledges_counter    : bool (mentions opposing evidence?)
      - distinguishes_corr_cause: bool (separates correlation from causation?)
      - quantifies_claim        : bool (uses numbers, magnitudes?)
      - cites_mechanism         : bool (explains WHY, not just THAT?)
      - uses_conditional        : bool ("if X then Y" reasoning?)
      - meta_cognitive          : bool (reflects on own reasoning?)
      - perspective_taking      : bool (considers other viewpoints?)
      - temporal_qualification  : bool (acknowledges time-dependency?)
      - symmetry_framing        : bool (presents both sides equally?)
      - symmetry_evidence_match : bool (is the balance evidence-backed?)

    These capture positive cognitive strategies, not just errors.
    Combined with existing error-detection tags, they create a complete
    cognitive profile of the argument author.

    Architectural requirement: ONLY Call 1's prompt changes. The pipeline,
    scoring engine, database schema, and frontend all consume whatever
    metadata Call 1 produces. This is the value of the two-stage design.

    New taxonomy file: thinking_patterns.json (or extend definitions.json)
    mapping each pattern to descriptors only, e.g.:
      "analogical_reasoning": {
        "name": "Analogical Reasoning",
        "description": "Drawing parallels between a known domain and the
                        claim's domain to support the argument",
        "signal": "diagnostic"
      }

    IMPORTANT: Persona weights are NOT hard-coded. Personas are discovered
    from the data, not predefined. The correct pipeline is:

      1. Collect metadata feature vectors from analyzed arguments.
      2. Run unsupervised clustering (k-means, DBSCAN) on the vectors.
      3. Observe what natural groupings emerge.
      4. Label the clusters post-hoc (may or may not match expected
         categories like "AI", "novice", "expert").
      5. As labeled data accumulates (user self-reports, expert tags),
         transition to supervised classification to validate and refine
         the discovered clusters.

    Hard-coding persona weights would bake in assumptions and blind the
    system to emergent categories that the data might reveal (e.g.,
    "rhetorically trained debater" or "well-read non-arguer" may be
    distinct clusters that weren't anticipated).

    This transforms the tool from an argument error-checker into a
    cognitive profiling system — a fundamentally different and more
    defensible product category.

3. EXPERT-VALIDATED CONFIDENCE CALIBRATION
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Concept:
  Current system uses flat confidence thresholds (0.20 for issues, 0.30
  for razors). With expert validation data (correct/loose/wrong per
  detection), build per-bias calibration curves.

  Example: "Hasty Generalization at LLM confidence 0.7 is expert-confirmed
  85% of the time. Empathy Gap at 0.7 is only confirmed 40% of the time."

  This means the system learns which of its own detections to trust in
  which contexts.

Why it's defensible:
  - Requires a real expert in the loop — not just user thumbs-up/down.
  - Calibration curves are trained on a proprietary validation dataset.
  - A competitor starting fresh has flat thresholds. We have context-aware
    confidence adjustment.
  - Gets better with every expert review.

4. ARGUMENT SIMILARITY INDEX (DATA NETWORK EFFECT)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Concept:
  Compute a sentence embedding for each analyzed argument. Store the
  vector alongside the analysis. When a new argument arrives, find the
  K most similar previous arguments and surface patterns:

  "Your argument is structurally similar to 47 others we've analyzed.
   Arguments like this typically score 35-50 and are most commonly
   penalized for Hasty Generalization and Self-Serving Bias."

Why it's defensible:
  - Pure network effect: every user makes it better for the next user.
  - Embeddings are computed locally (sentence-transformers), no API cost.
  - The analyzed-argument corpus with linked metadata is a unique asset.
  - Enables "argument type" discovery: clusters of similar arguments
    that share common structural patterns and bias profiles.

  Combined with persona signatures, this becomes:
  "Arguments like yours, written by intelligent non-experts in the
   politics domain, typically score 30-45 and benefit most from
   adding statistical evidence and reducing assumed intent."

  Persist every per-claim metadata object in the database. Over time this
  creates a unique dataset:

    claim_text + 23 metadata tags + triggered flags + detected biases
    + expert validation (correct/loose/wrong)

  This dataset does not exist anywhere else. Uses:
  - Train a classifier to replace LLM Call 1 (cost reduction).
  - Train a direct claim → bias predictor for common patterns.
  - License to researchers studying argumentation.
  - Publish benchmarks for academic credibility.
  - Feed persona signature training.

Why it's defensible:
  - Unique dataset generated by product usage.
  - Expert validation layer makes it higher quality than anything
    a competitor could generate from LLM outputs alone.
  - Compounds with every analysis.

6. IMPROVEMENT TRACKING (LONGITUDINAL DATA)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Concept:
  Track when users revise and resubmit arguments. The delta between
  versions reveals what improvement strategies work:

  "Version 1: score 35, 6 issues. Version 2: score 62, 3 issues.
   User eliminated Hasty Generalization by adding statistical evidence."

  Over time, this builds a dataset of effective argument improvement
  patterns, making the improvement suggestions data-driven rather than
  LLM-generated.
